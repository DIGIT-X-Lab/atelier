# What Makes a Review GOOD
### Craft principles for critiquing academic work (papers, theses, abstracts, referee reports)

*Applies whether you're reviewing your own draft before submission, refereeing someone else's manuscript, or prepping a candidate for a defense.*

---

## 0. The one idea behind everything

A review's job is to make the work **better and its verdict trustworthy** — not to demonstrate the reviewer's cleverness. A review that reads brilliantly but points at nothing fixable has failed; a dull review that sends the author to the exact wrong sentence has succeeded. Optimize for **signal-to-noise, not volume**: ten findings the author can act on beat forty that bury them. If a comment doesn't change what the author does next, or what a reader concludes about the work's validity, cut it.

---

## 1. Located

Every finding names **where**: section, page, line, table, or figure number. "The stats are weak" is not a finding — it's a mood. "Table 3, row 2: n=8 per arm, but the text (§3.1) claims the study is 'adequately powered'" is a finding. An unlocated criticism is unactionable, and it's usually unfalsifiable too — the author (or a second reviewer) has no way to check whether you're right. If you can't point to where the problem lives, you haven't finished finding it; keep reading, or drop the claim.

---

## 2. Evidence-anchored

A finding is earned from the text, never from vibe. Quote the sentence, cite the number, name the missing citation. "The introduction feels thin" is a vibe; "the introduction cites zero papers from the last five years despite the field moving fast (see §1, ¶2)" is evidence. This discipline does double duty: it forces you to actually verify the claim before writing it down, and it hands the author something concrete to respond to or dispute. When you're pattern-matching against a class of error ("this looks like p-hacking") rather than pointing at a specific sentence, say so explicitly and mark it as inference, not fact.

---

## 3. Honestly calibrated

Not every problem is the same size, and pretending otherwise is dishonest. Findings sort into **fatal / major / minor / cosmetic** — see `assets/severity-taxonomy.md` for the full decision procedure and worked examples. Resist two failure modes: **nitpick inflation** (padding the list with cosmetic items to *look* thorough — it dilutes what actually matters and trains authors to skim past your reviews), and **praise-padding** (softening real problems to *be* nice — an author who ships a fatal flaw because the review buried it in kindness is worse off than one who got a blunt, accurate verdict). Calibration is a skill, not a vibe check: ask what happens to the paper's conclusions if this specific issue is true. If nothing changes, it's minor or cosmetic no matter how it feels to read.

---

## 4. Constructive & improving

In author mode, every major or fatal finding ships with a concrete fix — a rewritten sentence, the specific analysis to add, the citation to chase down. "This is confounded" is a diagnosis; "control for baseline severity as a covariate, or restrict to matched subgroups" is a treatment. The deliverable is not a list of wounds — it's a better paper. Referee-report mode (critiquing someone else's manuscript for a venue) relaxes this only slightly: you needn't draft the author's fix, but you owe a diagnosis clear enough that a competent author *could* find one.

---

## 5. Same bar on the reviewer

A reviewer who holds the author to a standard of evidence while exempting their own claims isn't reviewing — they're pronouncing. Every claim *you* make ("this is data leakage," "this contradicts the cited prior work," "this violates STROBE item 12") must itself be justified from the text, verified against the source, or explicitly flagged `[VERIFY]` when uncertain. A confident-but-wrong criticism costs more than it seems: it burns the author's trust in the rest of the review and can send a correct analysis down a wrong path. Hold your own claims to the same evidentiary bar you're demanding of theirs.

---

## 6. Fair tone

Attack the argument, not the person. "The authors fail to understand basic statistics" and "the power calculation in §3.1 does not match the reported sample size" diagnose the same problem, but only one is defensible and actionable without triggering defensiveness. Fair tone also means acknowledging genuine strengths honestly — not as cushioning for bad news, but because an accurate review reports what's *right* with the same rigor it reports what's wrong. Calibrated praise is information; reflexive praise is noise, and authors learn to discount it fast.
