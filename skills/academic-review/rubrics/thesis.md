# Thesis & Dissertation Rubric
### The long-form bar: narrative arc, chapter coherence, the degree-level contribution, and defense readiness

*Loaded when `workType === "thesis"`. Examiner/committee mode reuses this file — the difference is the deliverable (a rigor pass plus likely defense questions), not the criteria. Read `rubrics/_principles.md` first; severity words are in [`../assets/severity-taxonomy.md`](../assets/severity-taxonomy.md). A thesis's empirical chapters are still manuscripts — apply [`manuscript.md`](manuscript.md) and the matching [`../standards/`](../standards/_index.md) pack to each results chapter; this file adds what the manuscript rubric doesn't cover: the whole-document arc and the degree bar.*

A thesis is judged as one sustained argument, not a stapled stack of papers. The failure modes that matter most at this length are structural — they live *between* chapters, in the seams a paper-length review never has to check.

---

## The narrative arc

A thesis should read as a single question pursued across chapters, each earning its place in that pursuit. Check the **through-line**: can you state, in one sentence, the central contribution, and does every chapter visibly serve it? A chapter that is good work but doesn't connect to the thesis's spine is a structural finding — either the framing needs to absorb it or it needs to go. Watch for the "three published papers in a trench coat" thesis: technically sound chapters with no connective argument, an introduction that doesn't set up what follows, and a conclusion that doesn't synthesize back. The introduction must promise the arc; the conclusion must deliver it and say what it *means*, not just recap.

## Chapter coherence & consistency

- **Does each chapter earn its place?** For every chapter, ask what the thesis would lose without it. If the answer is "nothing the argument depends on," it's padding — flag it. Length is not contribution.
- **Consistency of notation & terminology across chapters** — the same symbol must mean the same thing in Chapter 2 and Chapter 5; the same concept must not be renamed between chapters. Drift here is common when chapters were written months apart, and it quietly erodes an examiner's confidence. This is often minor individually but major in aggregate when it makes the argument hard to follow.
- **Cross-chapter dependencies** — a method introduced in Chapter 3 and relied on in Chapter 5 should be signposted; results should not contradict each other between chapters (a parameter reported one way early and another way later).

## The degree-level bar

The question an examiner ultimately answers: **is the contribution sufficient for the degree being claimed?** This is a calibration judgment, and it differs by degree — a PhD requires a **novel, substantial, defensible contribution to knowledge**; a Master's/MD thesis requires demonstrated competence in independent research and method, not necessarily field-changing novelty. Assess honestly against the *right* bar: state whether the contribution clears it, and if it's borderline, say where the weight is thin and what would strengthen it. Over-claiming the contribution's significance is a finding; so is a genuinely strong contribution buried under diffident framing that undersells it.

## Literature-review completeness

The literature chapter is where examiners probe first, because gaps are easy to spot. Check it is **current** (does it engage the last few years, or stop at the year the candidate started?), **fair** (does it engage work that competes with or complicates the thesis, or only supportive work?), and **critical** rather than a catalog (does it position the thesis in the field, or just summarize papers in sequence?). A missing key reference that a domain examiner will certainly know is a major finding — locate it precisely.

## Defense / viva readiness

The most useful thing this rubric produces for a candidate is a rehearsal of the hardest questions before the room asks them. An examiner's questions come predictably from the thesis's **weakest load-bearing claims** — so find those first, then turn each into a question.

### Generating likely examiner questions

Derive **8–12 probing questions** by working the thesis for its soft points:

1. **The unstated assumption** — for each central claim, what must be true for it to hold that the thesis doesn't defend? ("You assume your training cohort is representative of the deployment population — what's your evidence?")
2. **The methodological choice** — for each major method decision, why this and not the obvious alternative? ("Why this architecture / this statistical model / this cohort over X?")
3. **The boundary** — where does the contribution stop holding? ("For which patients / settings would your method fail?")
4. **The comparison** — how does this actually differ from the closest prior work? ("What does your Chapter 4 do that [known prior method] doesn't?")
5. **The weakest chapter** — the one with the thinnest results or the biggest leap gets a direct challenge to its central claim.
6. **The "so what"** — the significance question the conclusion must survive. ("If you're right, what changes for the field or the clinic?")

Draw each question from a *located* weakness in the thesis, not from a generic list — a good rehearsal question can be traced back to the exact page that provoked it. Give the candidate both the question and the strongest honest answer available from their own work (and flag where no strong answer exists — that's the finding to fix before the defense, not to paper over in the room).
