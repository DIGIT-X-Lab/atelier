# STARD — Standards for Reporting Diagnostic accuracy studies

*Encodes STARD 2015. A fast-start memory aid — confirm borderline calls against the live published checklist.*

## What it governs

Studies estimating the **accuracy of a diagnostic test** (or index model) against a reference standard — sensitivity, specificity, predictive values, likelihood ratios. Applies to imaging tests, biomarkers, and ML classifiers positioned as diagnostic tools.

## Highest-yield checklist items

1. **Reference standard** — what "truth" was the index test compared against, and is it an adequate gold standard? A weak or circular reference standard caps how meaningful the accuracy can be.
2. **Spectrum bias** — did the study population span the realistic range of disease severity and comparators, or contrast florid cases against healthy controls (which inflates accuracy)? Case-control ("two-gate") designs are especially prone.
3. **Indeterminate / uninterpretable results** — how were non-evaluable index or reference results handled? Silently dropping them inflates apparent accuracy.
4. **Blinding of interpretation** — were index-test and reference-standard results interpreted without knowledge of each other?
5. **Consecutive / random sampling** — were participants enrolled consecutively or randomly, or selectively (selection bias)?
6. **Partial / differential verification** — did all participants get the same reference standard, or only some (e.g., only test-positives verified)?
7. **Accuracy with uncertainty** — sensitivity/specificity reported with confidence intervals, and a 2×2 table available.

## Review prompts

- Is the reference standard genuinely adequate, and was it applied to everyone regardless of index result?
- Does the study population reflect the intended clinical spectrum, or does a case-control design inflate accuracy?
- What happened to indeterminate results — were they excluded in a way that flatters the test?
- Were index and reference interpreted blind to each other?
