# ERC Grant Proposals — Best-Practices Brief

*For Starting (StG), Consolidator (CoG) and Advanced (AdG) Grants, plus key differences for Horizon Europe collaborative proposals. Authoritative as of the **ERC Work Programme 2026** (calls opening July 2025 onward). Several rules change for **2027** — see "Recently changed." Always re-verify against the current Work Programme and the B1/B2 templates in the EU Funding & Tenders Portal, which are the only binding documents.*

Primary sources: https://erc.europa.eu/apply-grant/starting-grant · https://erc.europa.eu/news-events/news/changes-2026-and-2027-work-programmes · ERC Work Programme 2026 PDF · Guide for ERC Peer Reviewers.

---

## 1. The ERC mindset (read this first)

- ERC funds **frontier research driven by a single Principal Investigator** on the sole criterion of **scientific excellence**. No thematic priority, no mandated impact pathway, no required consortium, no co-funding. The opposite of Horizon Europe collaborative calls.
- The bet is **"high-risk / high-gain"**: if it works, the payoff is transformative; there is a real chance it does not fully succeed. A safe, incremental project that will obviously work is a *reject* even if flawlessly written.
- The single most common failure mode is **"a beautifully written proposal around an underdeveloped or merely-good idea."** Polish cannot rescue a non-ground-breaking concept. Develop the idea for months before writing.
- **Do not recycle** a national-grant or Horizon-collaborative proposal. The conceptual frame is different and panels notice.

## 2. The three schemes at a glance (2026)

| | Starting (StG) | Consolidator (CoG) | Advanced (AdG) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Eligibility (2026) | 2–7 yrs post-PhD | 7–12 yrs post-PhD | No time limit; established leader |
| Budget | up to €1.5M / 5 yrs | up to €2M / 5 yrs | up to €2.5M / 5 yrs |
| Extra (if justified) | +up to €1M | +up to €1M | +up to €1M |
| Track-record bar | "promise" / emerging independence | consolidating own group | "exceptional leader," ~last 10 yrs |
| Step 2 interview | Yes | Yes | **Yes (new in 2026)** |

Eligibility window can be extended for maternity/paternity leave, **parental leave (explicit from 2026)**, long-term illness, clinical training, national service, and **documented victims of violence (new 2026)**.

## 3. B1 vs B2 — exact structure and page limits

The proposal is split so that the **idea and the person** are judged before the **implementation**. Use the official templates from the submission system — do not rebuild them.

### Part B1 — submitted and evaluated at **Step 1** ("WHAT is this about, and WHO are you?")
- **Cover page** (title, synopsis, panel, keywords) — not counted.
- **Part I — the "extended synopsis" — max 5 pages.** Self-contained case for the idea: state of the art and the gap, the central objective/hypothesis, the overall strategy, and *why it is ground-breaking and ambitious*.
- **Curriculum Vitae + Track Record — max ~4 pages.** Evidence of capacity to deliver frontier research.

### Part B2 — submitted with B1 but evaluated only if you pass to **Step 2** ("HOW will you do it?")
- **Part II — max 7 pages** (StG/CoG/AdG; **10 pages** for Synergy). Detailed methodology, work plan/work packages, objective→method→outcome logic, **risks and contingencies**, timeline/Gantt, team roles, infrastructure.
- **Funding ID / appendices** — no page limit, *but must contain no hidden scientific content* (panels are told to ignore science smuggled here).
- **Budget / resources justification** — outside the page count.

> 2026 change: **feasibility is no longer assessed at Step 1.** All implementation, methodology, and feasibility detail lives in Part II (B2). Keep B1 about vision.

## 4. The evaluation: two elements, two steps, the marks

**The two evaluation elements (the only things scored):**
1. **The research project** — its **ground-breaking nature, ambition, and feasibility** (feasibility weighed at Step 2).
2. **The Principal Investigator** — **intellectual capacity, creativity, and commitment.**

There is no separate "impact" or "implementation" pillar as in Horizon Europe — excellence is everything.

**Step 1 (B1 only):** read by multiple panel members + remote referees, discussed in panel; a lead reviewer presents. **Marks: A / B / C.** A = strong enough to go to Step 2; B = some weaknesses, normally not retained; C = significant weaknesses, rejected. Only a limited number of A's pass — Step 1 is the major bottleneck.

**Step 2 (full B1+B2 + interview):** panel + external reviewers scrutinise methodology, feasibility, risk, team, resources; PI defends in a **Step-2 interview** (a scientific cross-examination, not a sales pitch). **Marks: A / B.** A = of sufficient quality to be funded if budget allows; B = weaknesses preventing funding. 2026 nuance: the **scientific proposal receives scores; the CV/Track Record receives qualitative feedback only** — weight shifts toward the *idea* over reputation.

> Panels rank on overall strengths/weaknesses, not arithmetic. Empirically, **weaknesses predict the outcome far more than strengths** — one fatal flaw sinks an otherwise strong proposal.

## 5. What "ground-breaking" and "high-risk / high-gain" must look like

The literal phrase has receded from the 2024+ forms in favour of "ambition" and "ground-breaking nature," **but the risk concept is fully embedded.**

- **The right kind of risk is *conceptual*, not *operational*.**
  - *Conceptual risk (wanted):* the hypothesis may be wrong; the new theory/paradigm may not hold; success is genuinely uncertain *even with unlimited resources*.
  - *Operational risk (unwanted):* "it might fail because we lack time/people/equipment." That is solvable with money and reads as poor planning. **"Fishing expeditions" (no hypothesis, just data trawling) are rejected.**
- **Ground-breaking = changes the field, not advances it.** New concepts, new questions, new methods, or working *between/across disciplines*. State explicitly *what becomes possible / what understanding changes if you succeed.*
- **The paradox you must satisfy:** conceptual high-risk AND a feasible approach. Aim for the "top-right corner" — bold, uncertain idea + a credible, concrete route to test it, anchored by **preliminary/pilot data** and demonstrated capacity.
- Make the **gain unmistakable:** name the breakthrough, the field-level consequence, the "if true, then everything downstream changes."

## 6. Section-by-section dos and don'ts

**Objectives** — Do: one crisp, ambitious overarching goal/hypothesis, broken into 3–4 coherent objectives/WPs that each carry risk and reward; measurable and falsifiable. Don't: a laundry list of 8 loosely-linked aims; obviously achievable objectives; "we will explore…" with no testable claim.

**State of the art** — Do: define the gap sharply and show *why it has resisted solution* and *why now / why you*; be current and global; cite likely reviewers and engage their work fairly. Don't: a textbook review; ignoring competing groups; overclaiming "no one has ever…".

**Methodology (B2)** — Do: tie every method to a specific objective; show objective→method→expected-outcome logic; include preliminary data; justify technique choices. Don't: vague "state-of-the-art methods"; methods implying the result is already known (kills the risk argument); detail so dense the multidisciplinary panel loses the thread.

**Risk & contingency (B2)** — Do: for each major risk, state likelihood, impact, and a **concrete fallback** that still yields meaningful results; distinguish intended conceptual risk from operational risks. Don't: claim "no significant risks" (signals it isn't ambitious) or hand-wave contingencies.

**Timeline / Gantt (B2)** — Do: a clear Gantt mapping WPs, milestones, deliverables, decision points, personnel over 60 months; show dependencies and go/no-go gates. Don't: an unrealistic, perfectly-linear plan; ignored hiring/lab-setup time; milestones with no decision logic.

**CV & Track Record (B1)** — Do: foreground *independence and leadership* for the career stage — best outputs (not just count), your role, prizes, invited talks, supervision, funding won, datasets/software/IP; use a **narrative** that argues "I am the person who can do *this*." Don't: an undifferentiated publication dump; failing to flag career breaks/field norms; a generic CV not connected to the project.

## 7. Most common reasons proposals fail

**At Step 1 (idea + PI):** idea is solid/interesting but **not ground-breaking** (incremental, predictable, or a "fishing expedition") — #1 cause; ambition asserted but not demonstrated; vague/unfalsifiable objectives; weak gap framing; track record not convincing for the stage; written for specialists only.

**At Step 2 (can you really do it?):** methodology too vague/generic or not matched to objectives; no preliminary data; feasibility doubts (unrealistic timeline, under-resourced, missing expertise); unconvincing risk/contingency — or ambition quietly engineered out to look "safe"; a **weak interview**; one identifiable flaw.

## 8. Tone, voice, writing for a multidisciplinary panel

- **The panel is your audience, not the world expert.** Only 1–2 readers are in your sub-field; the rest are excellent scientists from adjacent areas. Win them first.
- **Front-load the breakthrough.** First paragraph of B1: the big question, why it matters, what changes if you succeed — plain, confident language.
- **Layered writing:** accessible narrative on top, technical rigour beneath.
- **Active, assertive, precise.** "I will test whether X causes Y." Claim ground-breaking-ness explicitly; don't make the reader infer it.
- **Visuals earn their space:** one strong conceptual figure (the central idea), one Gantt; self-contained captions.
- **Define every acronym; no jargon walls.**
- **Ruthless clarity over completeness** within the 5/7-page limits.

---

## 9. Reviewer's eye / red flags (a referee's quick-reject list)

- "Interesting but incremental" — could appear in a normal national grant.
- No testable hypothesis / "we will collect data and see" → fishing expedition.
- Ambition claimed, never substantiated; no "if successful, the field changes because…".
- Methods imply the answer is already known → no real risk.
- "No significant risks foreseen" → not ambitious enough.
- Overclaiming novelty a referee can disprove ("first ever…").
- Objectives that don't map to methods that don't map to the timeline.
- CV is a publication dump; independence/leadership not evident for the stage.
- Science hidden in appendices/Funding ID to dodge page limits.
- Reads like a recycled Horizon/national proposal.
- Specialist-only prose the panel majority can't follow.

## 10. Pre-submission checklist

**Concept & fit** — [ ] genuinely ground-breaking, not incremental; [ ] risk is *conceptual*, the *gain* is stated explicitly; [ ] right scheme & eligibility window; [ ] not a recycled national/Horizon proposal.

**B1 (Step 1)** — [ ] Part I ≤ 5 pp, self-contained, breakthrough in the opening paragraph; [ ] sharp state-of-the-art + the unsolved gap; current/global citations; [ ] one ambitious goal → 3–4 falsifiable objectives; [ ] CV/Track Record ≤ ~4 pp, narrative argues capacity *for this project*, diverse outputs, career-stage-appropriate, breaks/field norms flagged.

**B2 (Step 2)** — [ ] Part II ≤ 7 pp (10 for Synergy); methods mapped to objectives; preliminary data; [ ] explicit risks with concrete, results-preserving contingencies; [ ] realistic Gantt with milestones, deliverables, go/no-go gates; [ ] budget justified and matched; resources/infrastructure credible; [ ] no scientific content hidden in Funding ID/appendices.

**Craft & compliance** — [ ] readable by a non-specialist; acronyms defined; [ ] one conceptual figure + Gantt, self-contained captions; [ ] official current-year templates, page/format limits met exactly; [ ] mock panel / colleague-from-another-field read done; [ ] Step-2 interview prep planned; [ ] resubmission-restriction status checked; ethics/security sections complete.

## 11. Horizon Europe collaborative — key differences

ERC ≠ Horizon Europe collaborative (RIA/IA/CSA). Do not transfer habits.

- **Three weighted criteria:** **Excellence, Impact, Quality & Efficiency of Implementation**, each scored **0–5 (half-points), threshold 3.0**, overall threshold 10/15. For **Innovation Actions, Impact ×1.5**. ERC scores excellence only.
- **Impact is mandatory and heavy:** pathways to impact, exploitation/dissemination, a **Plan for Dissemination, Exploitation and Communication**, **Key Impact Pathways**.
- **Consortium, not a single PI:** typically ≥3 independent legal entities from ≥3 different EU/associated countries; the **Implementation** section covers WPs, consortium composition, roles, management structure, risk register, budget table.
- **RIA vs IA = maturity:** RIA ≈ TRL 2–6 (research-led); IA ≈ TRL 6–8 (closer to market, industry/end-user presence). CSA = coordination/support, no new research.
- **Top-down vs bottom-up:** Horizon calls have **predefined topics and "expected outcomes"** you must hit; ERC is fully bottom-up. Off-topic Horizon = ineligible.
- **Bottom line:** Horizon rewards *fit-to-call, collaboration, and credible impact/delivery*; ERC rewards *individual scientific audacity*.

## 12. Recently changed / uncertain — verify before relying

- **2026 (in force):** feasibility assessed **only at Step 2**; **AdG now has a Step-2 interview**; CV/Track Record gets **qualitative feedback, not a score**; broadened "diverse research outputs"; parental-leave + victims-of-violence eligibility extensions; consolidated additional funding (up to €1M, or up to €2M for relocation).
- **2027 (announced, verify on adoption):** StG window → 0–10 years post-PhD; CoG → 5–15 years; max one StG and one CoG per career.
- **Resubmission restrictions:** outcome-dependent (historically a C at Step 1 or B at Step 2 triggers a 1–2 year lock-out). Check the current Work Programme.
- Budgets/page limits above are 2026 values — confirm in the live call.

> The only *binding* documents are the current-year ERC Work Programme, the Information for Applicants for the specific call, and the B1/B2 templates. Re-pull the WP year each cycle (this brief = WP 2026).
